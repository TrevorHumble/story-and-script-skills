# Skill 08 — Crisis Dilemma Analysis

## Purpose
The crisis is the story's moment of supreme decision. McKee is precise: it is a
TRUE DILEMMA — a choice between irreconcilable goods, or the lesser of two evils —
under the maximum pressure of the protagonist's life. It must be a static moment:
the audience held in the pain of the impossible choice before the climactic action
releases them.

A weak crisis produces an unearned climax. If the protagonist never truly suffers
the weight of an impossible choice, the resolution feels cheap regardless of how
well the climax is executed.

## Instructions

If a required input file is missing or does not contain the expected content, halt immediately and report the missing input to the orchestrator — do not proceed with partial context.

### Step 1 — Locate the Crisis
Find the moment of supreme decision immediately before the climactic action.
Describe it specifically. The crisis dilemma should force a choice that directly tests the value the controlling idea names — if the controlling idea is the story's argument, the crisis is the moment that argument is put on trial.

### Step 2 — Name the Two Choices
What are the two options the protagonist faces? Write them both explicitly.
Be specific to THIS story and THIS character — not "choose love or duty"
but "choose to give Sebastian the last of her gum or keep it and escape."

### Step 3 — Test for Genuine Dilemma
Is this a Best Bad Choice (two bad options) or Irreconcilable Goods (two good
options the protagonist can't both have)?
If one option is clearly better with no real cost, it is NOT a genuine dilemma.
The test: does the protagonist suffer choosing? Does the audience?

### Step 4 — Test Maximum Pressure
Is this the moment of most extreme pressure in the protagonist's life in this story?
If the protagonist has been in worse situations earlier, the crisis is misplaced.

### Step 5 — Test the Static Quality
Does the story pause at this moment of choice? Or does the protagonist decide
too quickly, before the audience has felt the full weight?
McKee: the audience must be kept in the crisis — held in the suffering — before
the climactic action releases them.

### Step 6 — Test Irreversibility
After the protagonist chooses, can they undo it? The climax that follows
must be irreversible.
