# Skill 13 — Negation of the Negation Mapping
# 📋 ONE-PAGER SUMMARY AFTER THIS EXERCISE

## Purpose
McKee's most demanding thematic concept: the negation of the negation is not
simply the opposite of the story's core value — it is that value in its most
corrupt form, a negative disguised as something good. It is one thing to be
imprisoned. It is another to be imprisoned by someone who claims to love you.

Stories that only move between positive and negative are operating in a limited
range. The most powerful stories descend into the negation of the negation —
where the worst thing is not simply bad but is a corruption of something good —
before the climax resolves or confirms that descent.

### Step 1 — Map the Thematic Square
Using the controlling idea's core value, map the four positions:
- **Positive**: the value fully achieved
- **Contrary**: the absence of the value
- **Contradictory**: the direct opposite of the value
- **Negation of the Negation**: the value corrupted — the negative disguised as positive

Example with core value of AUTONOMY:
- Positive: genuine self-determination
- Contrary: passivity / drifting
- Contradictory: control by others
- Negation of the Negation: believing you're free while actively dismantling yourself

### Step 2 — Map the Story's Progression
For each act (or major section in a short), identify where the protagonist's
situation falls on the thematic square.

### Step 3 — Test for Depth
Does the story descend into the negation of the negation before the climax?
If not: is the story pulling its punches? Is it avoiding the most painful
and most resonant version of its own territory?

### Step 4 — Test the Climax
Does the climax resolve the negation of the negation — either by achieving the
positive, or by confirming the tragic full descent?
A climax that stops at the contradictory (simply bad) without reaching the
negation (corrupted bad) may not have gone far enough.

### Step 5 — Check Other Characters
Do any supporting characters embody the negation of the negation as a permanent
state? The antagonist often lives there.
