# Skill 12 — Principle of Antagonism Stress Test

## Purpose
McKee: "The protagonist is only as strong as the forces that oppose them."
This is not about plot difficulty — it is about character revelation. Weak
antagonism means the protagonist is never pushed to their limit, which means
their true nature is never tested, which means the audience never truly knows them.

The antagonist's job is to NEARLY WIN. Not to be defeated easily, not to be
a nuisance — to come as close to defeating the protagonist as the story's stakes
demand.

## Instructions

If a required input file is missing or does not contain the expected content, halt immediately and report the missing input to the orchestrator — do not proceed with partial context.

### Step 1 — Identify All Forces of Opposition
List the primary antagonist and every significant force opposing the protagonist
(characters, institutions, internal conflicts, the world's logic itself).

### Step 2 — Test at the Climax
At the story's climax: is the protagonist at their absolute limit?
Have the forces of opposition brought them as close to total defeat as possible?
If the protagonist arrives at the climax with resources to spare, the opposition
was not strong enough.

### Step 3 — Test the Antagonist's Plausibility of Victory
Is there a version of this story where the antagonist wins?
If you cannot imagine the antagonist plausibly defeating the protagonist,
they are not strong enough. The audience must believe the protagonist could lose.

### Step 4 — Test Each Conflict Level
Inner antagonism: is the protagonist's self-opposition as powerful as the
external forces? Does it actively sabotage their pursuit of the want?
Personal antagonism: does the close-relationship conflict cut deep enough to
threaten something the protagonist genuinely cannot afford to lose?
Extra-personal antagonism: does the world or system the protagonist is operating
in have rules that genuinely constrain them?

### Step 5 — Apply the Strengthening Test
Mentally make the antagonist stronger, smarter, or more morally complex.
Now ask: does the protagonist have to work harder, risk more, change more deeply?
If making the antagonist stronger doesn't change what the protagonist must do,
the antagonist wasn't the real obstacle. Find what actually is.

### Step 6 — Test the Antagonist's Argument
Is the antagonist right about something? A fully dimensional antagonist is not
simply wrong — they have a point. The protagonist who defeats them must overcome
not just their force but their argument.
What is the antagonist's argument? Is the story engaging with it?
