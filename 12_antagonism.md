# Skill 12 — Principle of Antagonism

## Purpose

McKee: "The protagonist is only as strong as the forces that oppose them." This is not about plot difficulty — it is about character revelation. Weak antagonism means the protagonist is never pushed to their limit, which means their true nature is never tested.

The antagonist's job is to NEARLY WIN. Not be defeated easily, not be a nuisance — to come as close to defeating the protagonist as the story's stakes demand.

- `script.txt`
- `02_controlling_idea.md`

## Instructions

### Step 1 — Identify all forces of opposition
Primary antagonist plus every significant opposing force (characters, institutions, internal conflicts, the world's logic itself).

### Step 2 — Test at the climax
Is the protagonist at their absolute limit? Have the forces brought them as close to total defeat as possible? If they arrive with resources to spare, opposition was insufficient.

### Step 3 — Plausibility of victory
Is there a version of this story where the antagonist wins? If you cannot imagine plausible defeat, the antagonist is not strong enough. Audience must believe loss is possible.

### Step 4 — Test each conflict level
- Inner antagonism — as powerful as external forces? Actively sabotaging the want?
- Personal antagonism — close-relationship conflict cutting deep enough to threaten something the protagonist cannot afford to lose?
- Extra-personal antagonism — does the world/system have rules that genuinely constrain?

### Step 5 — Strengthening test
Mentally make the antagonist stronger, smarter, more morally complex. Does the protagonist have to work harder, risk more, change more deeply? If no change, the antagonist wasn't the real obstacle — find what is.

### Step 6 — Antagonist's argument
Is the antagonist right about something? Fully dimensional antagonists have a point. The protagonist who defeats them must overcome both the force and the argument. What is the antagonist's argument? Is the story engaging with it?

**Note for hollow-by-design antagonists.** If the controlling idea constructs the antagonist as a non-personal system (algorithm, institution, force-without-interior), Step 6's "argument" is the system's *operating logic*, not psychological motivation. Evaluate accordingly.

- **CLIMAX STRESS TEST** — pass/fail
- **PLAUSIBILITY OF VICTORY** — pass/fail

- **PROBLEMS** — scene-specific
- **SUGGESTIONS** — concrete strengthening per scene
- **TEACHING MOMENT** — 4-part structure; protagonist's strength depends on opposition's strength; why easy victories produce forgettable stories
