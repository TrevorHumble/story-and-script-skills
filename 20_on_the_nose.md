# Skill 20 — On-the-Nose Dialogue

## Purpose

On-the-nose dialogue is dialogue without subtext: characters saying exactly what they mean, feel, and want. McKee: "What people say is always a tactic to get what they want." People in conflict rarely express their true feelings — they use speech as weapon, shield, or distraction. On-the-nose dialogue assumes characters speak sincerely at their most conflicted moments — the opposite of reality.

Works in concert with Skill 19. Where Skill 19 maps the whole beat structure, this zooms into specific on-the-nose lines and finds the fix.

## Inputs

- `script.txt`
- `19_beat_analysis.md`

## Instructions

### Step 1 — Mark on-the-nose lines
Read the dialogue. Mark any line where the character is:
- Stating exact emotion: "I'm angry at you."
- Stating exact want: "I need you to help me."
- Stating exact fear: "I'm scared of being alone."
- Explaining their subtext to the other character: "The real reason I'm here is..."

### Step 2 — Redundancy test
For each marked line: if cut entirely, could the audience still infer the emotional state from surrounding behavior and context? If yes, the line is redundant AND on the nose. Both problems.

### Step 3 — Find the real want
For each on-the-nose line: what does the character ACTUALLY want here? What would they NOT say if they were a real person navigating this?

### Step 4 — Find the tactic
What indirect approach — what tactic — would serve the same want without stating it? Rewrite so the character pursues the want through action, not declaration.

### Step 5 — Check exposition for on-the-nose delivery
"As you know, Bob" lines — characters telling each other things they both already know purely for the audience. Flag each.

## Output

Save as `20_on_the_nose.md`:

For each on-the-nose line:
- Scene + character + exact line
- **Real want** — what they're actually after
- **Redundancy test** — YES (audience already knows) / NO (this is needed)
- **Suggested rewrite direction** — what tactic would serve the same want indirectly

Then:

- **EXPOSITION PROBLEMS** — As-you-know-Bob lines with scene refs
- **COUNT OF ON-THE-NOSE LINES**
- **MOST DAMAGING INSTANCE** — which line most undercuts its scene, why
- **PROBLEMS** — numbered
- **SUGGESTIONS** — concrete rewrites or rewrite directions (not "add subtext" but "instead of Florence saying 'I feel like I'm disappearing,' have her look at her hands and say nothing.")
- **TEACHING MOMENT** — 4-part structure; on-the-nose as trust failure; indirect approach respects audience intelligence
