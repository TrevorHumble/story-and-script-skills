# Skill 02 — Controlling Idea

## Purpose

The controlling idea is the story's irreducible meaning: a single sentence naming the value the climax brings into the world and the cause that produces it. It is not a theme word ("love") or a subject ("grief") — it is an *argument*, a falsifiable claim about how life works, proven by the events of the story.

This is the north star every downstream skill measures against. If the controlling idea is wrong or unclear, the whole analysis drifts.

## Inputs

- `script.txt`
- `01_genre_contract.md`

## Instructions

### Step 1 — Find the climax
Identify the climactic action — the final, most decisive, irreversible event. Write it in one sentence.

### Step 2 — Extract the value
What value comes into the protagonist's world as a result of the climax? State it as a single charged word: love, freedom, captivity, isolation, agency, survival, loss. Note positive or negative charge at the end.

### Step 3 — Extract the cause
What is the chief cause, force, or means by which this value arrives? What did the protagonist do, fail to do, or discover?

### Step 4 — Compose the sentence
Form: `[Value] [charges positive/negative] when [cause].`

### Step 5 — Test it
- Complete sentence with both value AND cause?
- Does the opening set up the conditions this idea will prove?
- Does every major scene serve or complicate this argument?
- Does the subplot support or ironically contradict it?

### Step 6 — Surface hidden arguments
Read the script again with fresh eyes. Is the story actually arguing something different than what you wrote? Sometimes the script knows something the writer hasn't consciously chosen yet. If you find a competing controlling idea, name it.

## Output

Save as `02_controlling_idea.md`:

- **CLIMAX** (one sentence), **VALUE** (word + charge), **CAUSE** (one sentence)
- **CONTROLLING IDEA** — the full sentence
- **TEST RESULTS** — pass/fail per test, with explanations
- **HIDDEN ARGUMENT** if found
- **PROBLEMS** — scenes/moments where the controlling idea breaks down
- **SUGGESTIONS** — concrete (not "clarify the theme," but "Scene X is pulling the story toward Y argument; consider Z")
- **TEACHING MOMENT** — 4-part structure; explain theme-as-word vs. theme-as-argument
