# Skill 11 — Character Dimension

## Purpose

McKee defines *dimension* as CONSISTENT CONTRADICTION — either within a character's deep nature (a ruthless person who is tender with strangers) or between surface and depth (a charming person who is destructive at their core).

The key word is *consistent*. A contradiction that appears once is a surprise. A contradiction that recurs under different pressures, in different situations — that is a dimension. It means the audience can never fully predict this person.

A flat character has no contradictions. An inconsistent character has random contradictions. A dimensional character has contradictions that are both surprising and inevitable.

## Inputs

- `script.txt`
- `10_true_character.md`

## Instructions

### Step 1 — List traits and behaviors
For each major character: every trait and behavioral pattern visible in the script. Both surface (characterization) and pressure-revealed (true character) from Skill 10.

### Step 2 — Find contradictions
Where do two traits or behaviors oppose each other? Not inconsistency — *psychologically real* contradiction. List each: "[Character] is [X] but also [Y]."

### Step 3 — Test for consistency
Does each contradiction recur? In different situations under different pressures? One appearance = surprise. Multiple = dimension.

### Step 4 — Test for credibility
Can you construct a psychological or biographical reason why these two opposing traits coexist in this person? If you cannot explain it, it may be inconsistency, not dimension.

### Step 5 — Test under maximum pressure
When things are hardest, which side of the contradiction wins? Untested contradictions are decoration, not load-bearing.

### Step 6 — Check the antagonist
A purely negative antagonist with no contradictions is a function, not a character. Does the antagonist have at least one dimension — a place they surprise us in a credible way?

**Important note for non-standard antagonists.** Some antagonists (the algorithm, the system, an institution) are deliberately constructed as *hollow* — the absence of interiority IS the controlling idea. In those cases, do not flag missing dimension as a writing problem. Check `02_controlling_idea.md` and `directors_notes.md` first; if the antagonist is staged as a non-personal force, evaluate accordingly.

## Output

Save as `11_character_dimension.md`:

For each major character:

- **TRAITS INVENTORY**
- **CONTRADICTIONS** — each: consistent (X appearances) / single, credible Y/N with explanation, tested under pressure Y/N
- **DIMENSIONALITY ASSESSMENT** — Dimensional / Flat / Inconsistent

Then:

- **ANTAGONIST DIMENSION** — specific contradiction + working? OR explicit note if hollow-by-design
- **PROBLEMS** — flat characters, random contradictions
- **SUGGESTIONS** — concrete (e.g., "X's contradiction appears in Sc 5 and Sc 7. Needs one more under different pressure to become a dimension; consider Sc 3.")
- **TEACHING MOMENT** — 4-part structure; inconsistency vs. dimension; why contradiction must be consistent to create inner life
