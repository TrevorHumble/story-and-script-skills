# Skill 10 — True Character vs. Characterization

## Purpose

*Characterization* is the surface — how a character dresses, speaks, what job they hold, how they act when nothing's at stake. *True character* is who they actually are, revealed only when they must make a meaningful choice under genuine pressure.

McKee: "True character is revealed in the choices a human being makes under pressure — the greater the pressure, the deeper the revelation."

A character can be richly characterized for fifty pages. If they are never tested, the audience does not know them.

## Inputs

- `script.txt`
- `02_controlling_idea.md`
- `03_story_spine.md`
- `09_conflict_levels.md`

## Instructions

### Step 1 — List characterization
For each major character: surface traits. How they appear, speak, what's known about them, what we observe in low-stakes moments.

### Step 2 — Identify moments of choice under pressure
For each character: every consequential choice with real cost. What gets revealed? Connect each revelation to what the controlling idea argues — true character and the central claim are ultimately the same thing.

### Step 3 — Test the pressure
Is there a REAL cost to one of the options? Risking something they genuinely value (relationship, safety, identity, moral self-image)? If no real cost, the pressure is insufficient — only characterization is being revealed, not true character.

### Step 4 — Surface vs. depth contradiction
Does behavior under pressure SURPRISE us in a way that's more true than the surface? Or do they behave exactly as their surface predicts? Most revealing: they turn out to be different from — but more true than — who they appeared to be.

### Step 5 — Deepest revelation
For each character: which choice reveals them most deeply? It should be near the climax — climax produces the deepest revelation.

### Step 6 — Arc
Does true nature change or become more fully expressed across the story? Or does the character reset after one pressure-test?

## Output

Save as `10_true_character.md`:

For each major character:

- **CHARACTERIZATION** (surface)
- **MOMENTS OF CHOICE UNDER PRESSURE** — each scene, choice, cost (real/insufficient), what's revealed
- **DEEPEST REVELATION** — scene + what it shows
- **SURFACE VS. DEPTH** — does pressure reveal something truer?
- **ARC** — true nature changes/deepens, or character resets?

Then:

- **PROBLEMS** — characters never genuinely tested, choices with no cost
- **SUGGESTIONS** — specific scenes where pressure could be increased or cost added
- **TEACHING MOMENT** — 4-part structure; characterization vs. true character; why a richly described character with no pressure is still a stranger
