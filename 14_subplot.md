# Skill 14 — Subplot Design

## Purpose

Subplots are secondary stories that begin in Act One and weave through the central plot. They must INTERSECT with the central plot — not run parallel to it. A parallel subplot splits the story; an intersecting subplot deepens its meaning.

For a short film, subplots are compressed or absent. If absent, note it. If present, every moment must earn its place.

## Inputs

- `script.txt`
- `02_controlling_idea.md`
- `05_act_structure.md`
- `13_negation.md`

## Instructions

### Step 1 — Identify all subplots
Every secondary story thread with its own arc. May be minimal or absent for a short.

### Step 2 — Check origins
Does each begin in Act One? A subplot that starts later feels like an intrusion.

### Step 3 — Check intersection
Does each INTERSECT the central plot at a crucial moment — actually affect it or be affected by it? Or just run alongside? If alongside, it's a parallel story, not a subplot.

### Step 4 — Check timing
Each subplot should climax and resolve before the central climax, or share it. A subplot extending past the central climax pulls attention from the resolution.

### Step 5 — Thematic function
Reinforces the controlling idea (same argument from a different angle), or ironically contradicts it (counterexample)? Thematically neutral = missed opportunity.

### Step 6 — Removal test
Remove the subplot entirely — does the central plot lose something irreplaceable? If no, it may not be earning its place.

## Output

Save as `14_subplot.md`:

- **SUBPLOTS IDENTIFIED** (or note if none)

For each subplot:
- Begins in Act One (Y/N)
- Intersects central plot (Y/N + where/how)
- Ends before/at climax (Y/N)
- Thematic function — Reinforces / Contradicts / Neutral
- Removal test — Irreplaceable / Removable

If no subplots: assess whether any secondary thread could add depth without diluting.

- **PROBLEMS** — scene-specific
- **SUGGESTIONS** — concrete
- **TEACHING MOMENT** — 4-part structure; parallel story vs. subplot; intersection (not coexistence) is the test
