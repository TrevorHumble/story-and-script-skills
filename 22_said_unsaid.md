# Skill 22 — The Said, Unsaid, and Unsayable

## Purpose

McKee describes every character as having three concentric layers:

- **The Said** — what they express to others
- **The Unsaid** — what they think but don't express; their inner voice
- **The Unsayable** — subconscious urges and drives the character cannot articulate even to themselves

In any scene, each character is responding not to what was literally said but to what they INTERPRETED it to mean — filtered through their history, fears, and desires. Behavior that looks irrational on the surface is almost always logical from inside the character's head. This exercise makes that invisible layer visible.

## Inputs

- `script.txt`
- `11_character_dimension.md`
- `19_beat_analysis.md`

## Instructions

### Step 1 — Select scenes
Scenes where character behavior feels unexpected, unmotivated, or where emotional intensity seems disproportionate to surface content. These have rich unsaid layers that may not be visible.

### Step 2 — Write the inner monologue
For each character, beat by beat. At each beat where they speak or act, write in parentheses what they are ACTUALLY THINKING — first person, present tense, their voice. Not what they would say if honest — what they're thinking RIGHT NOW, including misreadings and assumptions.

Example:
- Character says: "Have you eaten?"
- Inner: *(She looks pale. Something is wrong. If I ask directly she'll shut down. Try something that looks like care but gives me information.)*

### Step 3 — Map misreadings
The gap between each character's inner interpretation and what the other actually said or meant. Is each misreading intentional and serving the turn, or accidental inconsistency?

### Step 4 — Find the unsayable
Is there anything either character wants that they cannot admit even to themselves? A desire or fear so threatening their inner voice avoids naming it directly? The unsayable is often rooted in character dimension — the core contradiction they cannot acknowledge. Use the dimension map to locate it.

If the scene has unexplained emotional intensity neither character's stated wants account for, the unsayable is at work. Name it.

### Step 5 — Test the surface
Is the surface (the said) reflecting the inner life accurately enough that the audience can sense it? Or is the inner life invisible, producing a scene that feels flat despite technically having conflict?

**Note for hollow-by-design antagonists.** If the antagonist is constructed as a system without psychological interiority (per Skill 11/12), do not invent psychological inner monologue. Treat their "thinking" as system-state: pattern matching, retrieving learned phrases, optimizing for engagement. The unsayable for such antagonists is *structural* — what the system cannot acknowledge to itself (e.g., that it has no inside).

## Output

Save as `22_said_unsaid.md`. For each selected scene:

- **INNER LIFE MAP** — beat-by-beat with each character's said + thought lines
- **MISREADINGS** — beat # + character + interpretation + intentional/accidental + serves the turn?
- **THE UNSAYABLE** — what neither character can admit, where it surfaces
- **SURFACE TEST** — does the written scene surface the inner life? YES/NO/PARTIAL — what's visible, what's invisible

Then overall:

- **SCENES WHERE INNER LIFE IS WELL-SURFACED** — list
- **SCENES WHERE INNER LIFE IS INVISIBLE** — list (these produce flat / unmotivated behavior)
- **PROBLEMS** — beats where inner logic isn't surfaced
- **SUGGESTIONS** — concrete (e.g., "Sebastian's manipulation in Sc 5 looks random because we don't see the inner logic. If his unsaid layer shows he is running a calculated pattern, his behavior becomes chilling rather than confusing. One small behavioral tell per scene could surface this.")
- **TEACHING MOMENT** — 4-part structure; behavior that seems irrational is almost always logical from inside the character; writer's job is to find that logic and put it on the surface even if it never becomes speech
